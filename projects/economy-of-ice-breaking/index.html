Economy of Ice Breaking

The thesis investigates how contemporary economic and geopolitical interests in the melting Arctic are rooted in historical frameworks that conceptualize nature primarily as a set of extractable resources. Using the case study of Russia’s liquefied natural gas (LNG) strategy, the project traces a continuity between imperialist attitudes and current practices of environmental exploitation.

The analysis situates today’s capital-driven interventions in the Arctic within a broader historical narrative, examining how ideologies of control and extraction have evolved over time. While often framed as progress or development, these activities are shown to persist within a logic that contradicts environmental commitments made through international agreements. The thesis argues that such agreements frequently mask extractive ambitions behind a discourse of sustainability, contributing to a misleading representation of climate action.

By focusing on the Arctic—a region undergoing rapid transformation due to global warming—the work highlights how climate change becomes both a threat and an opportunity within capitalist frameworks. It critically examines how melting ice unlocks new trade routes and access to fossil fuels, reinforcing existing power structures and economic models rather than challenging them.

Through a combination of historical analysis, policy review, and case-specific research, the thesis exposes the ideological and material conditions that shape the Arctic’s contemporary role in global resource politics.
